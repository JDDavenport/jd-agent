How to Analyze a Case

From Strategic Management: Concepts and Tools for Real World Strategy

 

Case studies are a central part of a course in strategic management.  A case study provides an opportunity to address the strategy problems that actual organizations face.   As you attempt to understand and address strategic challenges, you will have the opportunity to apply the theoretical concepts, models, and tools you are learning in this course.  A well-written case and a good case discussion also does much more.  As you prepare to discuss a case in class you have the opportunity to apply the theories, concepts, and tools of the course to analyze the case as you break the issue down into individual parts/elements.  Good case preparation involves synthesizing the data in the case, or putting the individual elements that you’ve identified into an explanatory story about the case.   The case may also ask you to create or develop a course of action that the protagonist should follow, and to evaluate that course of action for its effectiveness. 

The skills of analysis, synthesis, creation, and evaluation constitute core critical thinking skills.  As you analyze a case, you stretch your mind and develop solid critical thinking skills. To do this effectively not only requires the proper use of logic, but also involves applying theoretical concepts, models, and tools to provide guidance with regard to the appropriate course of action. These skills will help you perform well in your strategic management class, but they are also the basis of success in your future job.  Case studies are a valuable learning tool and in what follows, we’ll lay out a systematic process that will help you develop your case analysis skills.  Here’s our time-tested process for effective case preparation:

  1. Begin by reading the case through once, carefully and thoroughly. The goal of this initial reading is to get a broad overview of the company and its strategy, story line, and data contained in the text and the exhibits. Take note of key elements of the firm’s strategy.  You should note the product and geographic markets the company competes in and their attractiveness, its value proposition to customers, the firm’s strengths—in other words, its resources and capabilities—that allow it to deliver its value proposition; or weaknesses that make it difficult to deliver its value proposition, and any factors that may make it difficult, or easy, for competitors to imitate its strategy. 

 

  1. With a strong general sense of the case and the firm’s strategy, answer these three questions to frame your analysis:

 

  • What is the situation the company/ individual is experiencing at the end of the case? The situation is a short statement of the overall state of the world the company finds itself in.  For example, if you were doing a case on Exxon-Mobil and its strategy for growth, the situation may be “Exxon-Mobil has successfully competed in the highly volatile petroleum extraction industry for decades.  It continues to act as an industry leader and has the largest market share of the oil exploration market.”  The data and narrative in the case help you identify the situation, especially a description of the industry/firm’s history and the drivers of its current and past financial performance.

 

  • What’s the complication? The complication creates the core challenge or opportunity and the need for action in the case.  It describes a barrier the company/ individual must overcome in order to continue to succeed or opportunity it must capture in order to enhance performance.  In our example above, the complication might be “However, new potential fields for petroleum extraction face increasing stakeholder resistance to development.”  The presence of stakeholder resistance complicates Exxon’s business model; resistance means that Exxon can’t continue to acquire and develop oil fields as it always has.  Something needs to change.  Depending on the type of case (see below), there may or may not be a complication, but you should always look for one.

 

  • What’s the central question or problem in this case? The question follows naturally from the combination of the situation and complication. The question or problem becomes the starting point and focus of all other work you will do in the case. Returning to the example above, if we know that Exxon has been successful for many years, but now faces increasing stakeholder resistance, one question might be “how should Exxon work with its stakeholders to insure access to new fields for petroleum extraction?”  Another valid question might be “given stakeholder resistance, where should Exxon search for new energy sources beyond petroleum extraction?” There are usually a number of primary questions that could frame a case. Solid preparation entails listing 3-4 potential core questions, and then making a decision about which one(s) you feel is most important.  You should come to class prepared to defend your choice of the most important question.    

 

  1. Once you have answered these three questions, you can proceed to the next question: “What type of case is this?” There are two broad types of cases you’ll be asked to prepare: Diagnostic and/or Decision. Once you understand what type of case you are dealing with, you can more effectively target your preparation.

 

  • The Diagnostic, or post mortem case. In this case you are given a story, facts, and details about history, either generally about the company or about a specific incident.  The objective in a diagnostic case is to understand “Why” or “How” something happened.  You play the role of investigator and your objective is to figure out “why” this company was or was not successful, either generally or in the specific situation.  The goal of a diagnostic case is to explain “why;” so it’s possible that there may be no clear complication in the case.  You might be able to understand the success or failure of the company just by identifying and then deconstructing the situation. A diagnostic case about Exxon might focus, for example, on why Exxon has been the leader in petroleum extraction for many decades.  The case, and accompanying data, would point out the different factors that contributed to Exxon’s success over the years. 

 

  • The Decision, or situational case. In this case type you are given a general story about the company, industry, and competitive situation. You will be given more detailed information about a particular issue, and the protagonist must make a decision about what to do and how to act.  Thus, your first step is to define the problem.  Decision cases will always have a complication, and the focus of the case is usually on how to overcome the challenge presented by the complication. There are two sub-types of Decision Cases. 
    • The “closed” decision case. The protagonist or central actor in the case finds himself/herself asking “should I choose A or B?”  The decision options are known and your job is to decide between different courses of action.  An example of a closed decision case for Exxon would be whether or not to pursue and develop a particular country or field for exploration.
    • The “open” decision case. The protagonist faces a critical issue and asks “what should I do?”  The decision options are unknown and your job is to create and evaluate alternative courses of action. In our Exxon example, if the protagonist had been asked to develop a strategy for stakeholder engagement, there would be several different ways he or she could design such a program.

In short, Diagnostic cases will always have a “why question” as the core question in the case.  Decision cases will always have a “what question” at the center of the case.  Please be aware that almost every Decision case has a diagnostic component (You can’t effectively think about “what to do now” until you understand “why we are here.”).  Moreover, almost every Diagnostic Case can be turned into a decision case by asking what the company should do next, or whether you agree with the past strategic decisions that have been made. 

  1. Identifying the key problem or question to be answered is the first step in a five-step process for tackling a case. Below is a quick overview of the 5-step process. 
  • Define the problem or key question to answer,
    1. How can the company ignite or sustain growth?
    2. What should the company do about a new competitor?
    3. Why has the company been so successful? (or failed?)

 

  • Develop candidate solutions to the problem (Example problem: How can a company ignite or sustain growth?)

 

Candidate solutions …

  1. Take existing products to new customer segments or geographies
  2. Change product features to appeal to a new segment
  3. Acquire a company that provides new capabilities

 

  • Test solutions by identifying key assumptions (What assumptions would have to be true in order for us to believe this is the right answer? If any of those assumptions is false, the solution is wrong—so you must search for a new solution.

Solution: Take existing products to new customer segments

  • Assumption 1: Demand for the company’s product would exist in the new customer segment
  • Assumption 2: The company has the volume capacity to serve the segment

 

  • Develop supporting data, analysis, or logic (Find support for the key assumptions upon which your solution rests. Employ quantitative or qualitative analysis, or use logic/theory to find support for assumptions).

 

Assumption: Demand for the product exists in the customer segment

  • Support: Other firms selling products in the new customer segment have grown 20% over past 3 years
  • Support: Customer surveys show preferences for the company’s particular product features over competitor products in the new target customer segment

 

  • Develop a point of view into a logical argument/presentation (note: this is most relevant when needing to prepare a written or oral case analysis).
  1. Introduction
  • S: Describe situation: “Historical growth has been exemplary …”
  • C: Introduce a complication: “However, growth has stalled over the past three years …”
  • Q: This implies a question, the key business problem: “How can the company ignite or sustain growth?”
  • A: State answer/solution and reasons why: “To resolve this issue, the company should _________ because ________ …”
  1. Provide supporting logic and analysis
  2. Key takeaways or recommendations

This 5-step process will help you effectively analyze a case and create reasoned answers to questions raised in the case.  We provide more information on each step below.

  1. Define the Problem. The most important problems are those that have the greatest influence on a company’s profitability/performance.  As previously suggested, examples of problems that you may encounter in a case include:  How can the company ignite or sustain growth?  What should the company do about a new competitor?  Once you haven formulated the core question or problem, brainstorm for a few minutes about other important questions and issues. Start by asking “what questions do I need answers to in order to answer the primary question?” Be sure to choose questions that include all the potential issues that feed into the main question or problem.  These questions form the backbone of your preparation strategy.  You want questions to be mutually exclusive (they don’t ask for the same information to get an answer), and collectively exhaustive (the questions cover all the important potential areas for analysis.  This will help you to ensure that you have identified the key problem/question that needs to be addressed as well as the questions that feed into that larger question/problem.

 

  1. Develop Candidate Solutions. For the Diagnostic case (or portion of the case) where the primary question is something like: “why has this company been so successful?” we would begin by identifying all the potential drivers of success. These are the candidate solutions.  Exxon, for example, might have succeeded because of external factors, internal factors, or luck.  For each of these categories, you should identify potential contributors.  If we looked at external factors for Exxon, we might list 1) macroeconomic factors, 2) industry attractiveness, and 3) competitor actions.  Under the internal heading, we might identify unique resources and capabilities (e.g., capability to find rich oil fields), values and priorities, or strategic approaches like innovation, cost leadership, acquisitions, or strategic alliances.  If you draw these factors out on a piece of paper, you’ll see a hierarchy, or issue tree, that captures all the questions you need to ask in order to answer the core question in the case. 

 

For a Decision case (for example, one where Exxon is facing a problem of how to best manage specific stakeholder groups to elicit their support for a strategic initiative), you would need to understand several additional things about Exxon and its stakeholders in order to figure out how Exxon might, or should, address this particular problem.  Who are the stakeholders and what are their “stakes?” What’s the history of Exxon’s interaction with its stakeholders (confrontational or cooperative)?  What organizational or market resources does Exxon have available to it to manage stakeholders?  How do Exxon’s competitors manage their stakeholder relationships?  What are the power bases and concerns of important stakeholder groups?  As with the analytical case, you’ll create a hierarchy, or option tree, that describes the questions you need to answer to inform your decision.

 

  1. Test solutions by identifying key assumptions. For each candidate solution identify what assumptions would have to be true in order for this to be the right answer.  As you identify each assumption that would need to be true, also identify what strategy theoretical concepts, models, data, and tools would provide support for those assumptions behind each of the candidate solutions.    For example, if a relevant question is the attractiveness of the petroleum extraction industry (perhaps because a firm is looking to enter the industry or is considering exiting the industry), then the 5-forces model and the 5 forces tool should be listed as models and tools that you can use to test your assumption that this is (or is not) an attractive industry to enter or exit.  Some cases will focus on a single tool or model (e.g., industry structure), but other cases will require you to use multiple models.

 

  1. Develop supporting data, analysis, or logic. Once you have identified the assumptions that would need to be true to support a candidate solution (and relevant models and data) you can do the appropriate analysis to test the assumption.    For example, descriptions of Exxon’s competitors, their market shares, strategies, and recent actions would help you fill in data on the “competitive rivalry” force in the five forces model.  Analyze data to test your assumptions and provide support for the solution you propose (for example, if you propose that Exxon should enter the wind or solar energy industries, you would want to use the “5 Forces” model and analyze data on the profitability/attractiveness of those industries).

 

Sometimes the data will be straightforward and can be found in the text of the case like diamonds in an open field.  The case might tell you, for example, the costs of extraction, such as drilling, rig assembly, transportation, and maintenance costs.  Other times, you will have to exercise judgment to make inferences.  For example, the text of the case may tell you that Exxon initiated four new extraction projects in the last year, and their total cost of exploration development was $1 Billion.  You could then infer that each field cost roughly $250 Million to develop.

 

As a general rule, Decision cases invite you to use your judgment and make inferences more often than Diagnostic cases do.  That’s because decisions are often made in the face of limited or incomplete information and the case writer wants you to exercise your judgment muscles.  

Collecting the data and putting it into the models is often the most time consuming step, but it is the basis of the insight you will gain as you prepare the case. 

 

  1. Develop a point of view into a logical argument/presentation. After you have considered each of the candidate solutions and the extent to which the underlying assumptions are true (supported by your logic/analysis) you should select what you feel is the strongest of the candidate solutions.  To do this it helps if you evaluate each solution/model independently and in conjunction with others.  We might find, for example, that the petroleum extraction industry is not particularly attractive; it may have high barriers to entry but be subject to high levels of supplier power, buyer power, substitutes, and competitive rivalry.  This would suggest that industry attractiveness is not the primary source of Exxon’s successful performance over the years.  You would then look at the results of the Company Diamond or firm-specific resources and capabilities as an explanatory factor. This analysis might suggest that Exxon succeeds because it has marshalled and deployed a unique set of valuable resources which far outweighs the attractiveness of the industry as a factor explaining the company’s success. 

 

Similarly, the Company Diamond model may help you see that Exxon does not seem to value stakeholders other than shareholders and the company has no capability for identifying relevant stakeholders and their concerns, or in responding proactively to stakeholder issues.  The Company Diamond model would suggest some potential courses of action for Exxon to use in managing stakeholder relationships.

 

As you use the outputs of the models to answer the primary question or problem, begin to think in terms of a story line.  How does the analysis you’ve done begin to fit together into a narrative that answers the primary question in the case?  We often recommend using the following framework:

  1. a) Situation (what is the situation that the company finds itself in?),
  2. b) Complication (what is the complication arising from the situation?),
  3. c) Question (what is the primary question that needs to be answered?),
  4. d) Answer (what is the answer you propose and why?).

 

In the examples above, a storyline focused on explaining why Exxon has been successful in the energy industry might include minor elements of the external environment that contribute to Exxon’s success, but would focus primarily on the company’s development of internal resources and capabilities as the main factor explaining its strong performance.  While this is the main “plot,” Exxon may have used acquisitions and alliances to enhance its resource base, or it may be particularly good at navigating different national environments.

 

For the Decision case, the story might be that Exxon has done little to foster stakeholder relationships. Then you must decide whether and how they can develop this capability, or the story might be that Exxon should put its efforts into developing new sources of energy rather than search for more oil.   

 

The story you develop becomes the basis of your contribution to the case discussion.  Of course you will use the evidence, data, and analytical work you have done to move the discussion forward, but the really strong contributors to case discussions—and similar decisions in the workplace—are those who have crafted a fact and evidence based story.   

 

Complete your preparation by stepping back and asking “so what?”  For a Decision case, this is the most critical step to adequate preparation.  So, what does the story you have developed mean for how the protagonist should move forward?  If you have developed a strong story line, then the “so what” question should help you develop a clear implication for action.  If the story is, for example, that Exxon needs to remain in petroleum exploration, but has low quality stakeholder relationships, then you should be able to identify some specific steps Exxon could take, over the short and long term, to improve its stakeholder relations and its capability in that area.

 

For a Diagnostic case, the “so what” question helps you identify the key takeaways from the case that will inform your future actions.  You might learn, for example, how Walmart or Southwest Airlines gained a cost advantage relative to competitors—which will provide you some practical concepts that can be applied to achieve cost advantage.   You might learn that Exxon’s competitive success arises largely from internally developed firm resources, but also as a result of deft acquisitions and alliances.  The takeaway might be that effective strategies in complex industries employ multiple levers (and layers) of competitive advantage.   It may also be that specific practices for identifying valuable acquisition or alliance targets are important as vehicles for enhancing a firm’s resource and capability base.   “So what” will help you crystalize the success (or failure) factors you observe in the case, and these can usually be rephrased as more general principles of strategy that you can use. 

If this sounds like a lot of work, it is!  You should allocate about 2-3 hours to prepare a case for a 60-80 minute discussion.  However, our experience is that diligently applying this case preparation process will improve your performance during case discussions, enhance your learning, and make the case study process an enjoyable part of your business education.   It is important to note that an important part of the case process is for you to gain experience articulating your ideas in front of others who may have a different point of view.  Management is a verbal occupation!  Others may want to argue your points before accepting your conclusions.  This is useful experience as it mirrors how actual decisions are often made in the business world.   As a result, using the case process will help improve your critical thinking skills and abilities that will benefit you long after you have finished your course in strategic management.

 

Good Luck!!
