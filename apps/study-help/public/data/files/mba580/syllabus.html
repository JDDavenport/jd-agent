Course Description

Business strategy is fundamentally about helping organizations achieve superior performance relative to competitors. At its core, it is about devising and implementing strategies to create and capture value, pinpointing and addressing critical obstacles, and identifying and evaluating potential future opportunities. Strategy requires taking the perspective of a top manager to understand how profitable business strategies can be designed, implemented, and maintained. This course consequently necessitates a holistic approach that puts many other functional pieces of business into a larger context. Thus, while we will not cover marketing, accounting, operations management, and so forth directly in this course, we will draw on theories and tools in these areas to deal with bigger picture issues faced by organizations.

Pedagogically we will proceed down two distinct paths. First, we will focus on developing strategic thinking skills, including skills in problem formulation, critical thinking, and strategic decision-making. In most managerial environments managers encounter ill-structured, complex, difficult to solve problems. An over- (or under-) abundance of information and limited time make addressing these problems challenging. To generate “good” strategy, managers must be able to diagnose—to develop a problem formulation that “defines or explains the nature of the challenge… that simplifies the often-overwhelming complexity of reality in identifying certain aspects of the situation as critical.”[1] They then must think in a structured and careful way to craft a solution to this problem. The cases, final project, and class learning approach have been designed to provide opportunities to develop and refine these strategic thinking skills.

The second path develops competence in understanding and applying strategy concepts, frameworks, theories, and tools used to formulate and evaluate business strategy. Without this deep understanding you will constantly be reinventing the wheel, which will limit your effectiveness at designing, implementing, and maintaining sound business strategies. Strategic tools and frameworks have been developed to assist in strategic decision making and to help managers identify and organize essential information that can be used to evaluate potential actions or decisions. Our focus here will result in you learning and memorizing the language, theories, and tools of strategy.

The melding of these two paths this semester will allow us to achieve our learning objectives. We will seek to develop the ability to ask and answer the “right” questions to formulate, implement, and rejuvenate business strategies. To paraphrase Carl von Clausewitz, a great Prussian military thinker, strategy is not a field in which rules or theories can be developed that should be dogmatically followed. Instead, “theory serves talented practitioners by expanding their powers of observation and sharpening their instinctive sense of judgement in action.”

[1] Richard P Rumelt (2011), “Good Strategy, Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters,” p 77.

 

Learning Objectives

At the end of this course, you should be able to:

  • Apply Strategic Theory and Tools: Employ strategic frameworks and tools to formulate, evaluate, implement, and rejuvenate business strategies.
  • Think Like a Strategist: Utilize principles of critical and strategic thinking to identify, formulate, and solve complex strategic problems both individually and in teams.
  • Analyze External Influences: Assess how external competitive environment factors influence long-term firm performance and average industry returns.
  • Evaluate Internal Resources and Activities: Examine how firm-level resources, capabilities, and activities can combine to deliver unique value.
  • Identify and Respond to Strategic Risks and Opportunities: Recognize common technological and competitive risks and opportunities and understand how firms can respond innovatively to achieve long-term advantages.
  • Communicate Strategic Ideas: Clearly articulate strategic ideas and recommendations verbally, in writing, and in presentations. Critique others’ strategic ideas constructively and effectively.
  • Collaborate Effectively: Work effectively in a team to provide value to a company.
  • Approach Strategy with Discipleship: Understand the differences in approaching strategy and strategic leadership from a position of discipleship and faith.
  • See Growth in Your Character and Faith: Notice that we have grown together in character and testimony as followers of Jesus Christ.

These course learning objectives coincide with the mission of the MBA program, the Marriott School of Business, and with the overall mission and aims of Brigham Young University.

Course Structure[2]

It is often said that experience is the best teacher. The case method packs more experience into an hour of learning than any other approach. That is why it forms the basis of learning in many prestigious MBA programs around the world, including our own. Case studies provide an immersive experience, bringing students as close as possible to real business situations in many different contexts. Judgment and experience lie at the core of managerial success. The case study method is extremely useful in building these leadership skills. Most of our class sessions will consist of one case discussion. Preparation of the day’s cases is essential, both for your grade and to get the most out of the course (see below). While each case provides an opportunity to study a variety of problems, our objective will not be to learn everything we can from a single case. Instead, we will focus on a few key problems and takeaways from each case, and study multiple cases.

 

Case discussions depend on the active and effective participation of students. The quality of a session is directly influenced by the thorough preparation of each student. You consequently must get involved and take a great deal of—in fact, the primary—responsibility for your learning. For us to have success using this method, you should be present, on-time, prepared, and ready to participate.

 

For each session I will post case-preparation questions, which will help prepare you for our discussion. These will also be some of the questions I use in my cold calls in class. To prepare for each case I suggest you 1) read the case-preparation questions, 2) consider which lenses, concepts, and tools are likely important to use in the case, 3) read through the case, 4) perform needed analyses to identify key strategic problems in the case, and to understand potential solutions, and 5) write out concise answers to each of the case preparation questions. While preparing you should focus on applying strategic tools, theories, concepts, and frameworks whenever possible (particularly those noted in the prompt) and use the data and other information provided in the case. If you are just sharing opinion without justifying it with data and analyses, you are not there yet. Like the “real” world, you will not be told exactly what analyses to run or what to focus on. Rather, you will be expected to think critically and creatively about what information can be brought to bear to support an argument, solve a problem, or make a decision. I have provided additional helps on Canvas that will help you if you have had limited experience with the case method.

 

I strongly encourage you to discuss and work on the cases before class with your teams and classmates. Articulating your views and having these views challenged by your peers will complement and reinforce what you learn in the classroom. You will take away much more from this course (and your MBA experience in general) if you prepare cases this way. Remember, our goal is to transform you in the BYU MBA program. Consequently, I invite you to embrace opportunities to grow in this course!

 

Finally, unless clearly specified on the assignment, use of generative AI on case writeups is strictly prohibited. Any use of generative AI will result in a zero on the case preparation assignment and may result in additional cheating penalties as outlined in the section below on academic honesty. As we will discuss in class, this policy is important to fostering a transformative experience.

Assignments (15% of final grade): Several assignments will be given throughout the course to support the course’s learning objectives. These will be individual assignments, although you can discuss the content of these assignments with your teams and others in the class. The final submission should be your own work. While the anticipated assignments are outlined in the schedule below (and online on Canvas), I reserve the right to add or drop assignments depending on class needs. These assignments are nested in the following categories:

  1. Pre-Class Quizzes: On days with pre-class quizzes, you are required to complete a quiz on Canvas before the start of class to earn full credit. These quizzes often will contribute to the case discussion.
  2. Case Question Write-ups: On days with case question writeups, you will be required to submit answers to one or more case preparation questions prior to class startin These writeups will often ask you to apply strategic frameworks and tools and will again be used during the case discussion.
  3. Strategy and Your Life Memo: You will be asked at multiple times during the semester to make entries to a personal strategy journal. These entries push you to apply strategic tools, concepts, theories, and frameworks to your own life. More info on this assignment will be posted to Canvas.
  4. Presentation Evaluations: At the end of the semester, during group presentation days, you will be required to provide feedback on your colleagues’ final project presentations.

 

Class Participation, Preparation, and Cold Calls (30% of final grade): As with many MBA-level courses, a significant portion of your final grade will be determined by class participation. To determine your final class participation score, I use data based on your class attendance, your performance during cold calls, your general engagement in the class including the quality of your comments, and your self-reported class participation.

 

  1. Attendance (50% of class participation score): Your participation will be assessed based on your class attendance. Your presence in class influences not only your own learning, but also the learning and performance of your teams and other classmates. Consequently, I seek to reward those that show up to class and to penalize those that do not. Because I realize you have important strategic tradeoffs to make in how you spend your time, I allow you twounexcused absences (for any reason) before absences start to negatively influence your participation grade. For extraordinary cases I will also excuse absences, resulting in no negative effect to your grade. To qualify as an extraordinary circumstance (and thus merit excusal), your absence should be caused by something (a) that you did not foresee, and (b) for which you could not avoid. Being in a traffic accident on the way to class or having a sudden family health emergency are examples of these circumstances. On the other hand, a client meeting, conference, job interview, or a date with your significant other are not extraordinary circumstances for which I am willing to make special arrangements, and instead count towards the two unexcused absence limit. After you have exhausted your two free absences, each unapproved absence will lower your attendance participation score by 5 percent. For all absences you will still be accountable for the information presented in class that day. I strongly encourage you to reach out to your team for notes and takeaways on days you are absent. If you decide to miss a class, I will assume you are making a strategic choice, and will be supportive! This does not mean, however, that you will be entitled to participation points or excusal for the absence, given the goals of the class. If you come late or leave early (regardless of how late or early), you will receive a 50% deduction for that day’s attendance points.

 

  1. Cold Call Performance (20% of class participation score): I generally will cold call at least one person to open each case, and a combination of open discussion and cold calling will follow. I will often cold call 4-5 students each class session. Please bring your name cards to each class to ensure we give you full credit for your participation. Cold calling allows you to practice taking a stance based on the theories and concepts you have studied for the day, and your analysis. I want you to have opinions and then to stand behind those opinions, and to practice delivering those opinions in succinct and careful ways that draw on the tools, concepts, theories, and frameworks we are learning. Cold calls are an opportunity to shine as an individual, and to provide evidence of your preparation for the case.

 

You should expect to be cold called about 2 times during the semester. My cold calls are mostly random, but I occasionally adjust the random outputs if the outputs seem inconsistent with my learning objectives for the class (e.g., a student being chosen multiple times in a short period of time). Each day I will enter class with a “cold call list” and will plan to call about 5 students throughout the class session.  

 

If you want to opt out of the potential to be cold called so that I don’t waste our time (and potentially embarrass you on days you haven’t prepared), you must let me know before the start of class. I give you one grace day to opt out of the potential for a cold call without any negative repercussions for your participation grade.

 

My expectation is that you will be prepared for the cold call (unless you opt out), that you will work hard during the moment to take a stand and convince others of your point of view, and that you will persist through the discomfort of the experience as others join the discussion. The best responses demonstrate that you have carefully applied the strategy frameworks and tools for the day (and prior days) to the case, and that you have tried hard to generate valuable insights.

 

In conjunction with the TAs, I will evaluate your cold call by categorizing your performance in one of the following ways:

 

EXCELLENT: Meets “Good” standard below, but also stimulates additional substantive and deep conversation. This is typically because you contribute an exceptional insight that shows deep engagement with the question and material. Often such insights come from insightful quantitative analyses of the case facts, and by bridging and integrating material from multiple class sessions in an insightful way. Usually this stimulates new discussion (often with many hands going up following your comment).

 

GOOD: Your comment shows you have read the case and thought through it carefully. You can apply frameworks, theories, and tools to the case. I generally assume that most prepared students in the room would have said what you said. This is the MOST common outcome for prepared students.

 

WEAK: While you have read the case, your understanding is at a superficial level and consequently your answer adds little insight to the case discussion. These answers do not generate substantive follow-on discussion, but instead lead to others wanting to answer the same question in a more complete way. You provide only weak justification for your comment using class concepts, frameworks, and quantitative analysis.

 

BLUFF: As we progress through the cold call it becomes clear that you have not done the minimum work required to prepare for the cold call. This includes not having a basic understanding of case facts and the key challenges and opportunities outlined in the case. I hope this is also an UNCOMMON outcome.

 

MISSING: If you are not present for the cold call, you will be classified as missing. I will place your name on the list to again be cold called on future days. If you have used up your two unexcused absences, then you will receive 0 pts for this cold call.

 

To succeed in cold calls, remember that silence is your friend. It may be uncomfortable to take a moment of silence to gather your thoughts but remember everyone wants you to be successful! Second, persist and don’t give up! Many times, the answers are not clear (or there are not obvious right answers) in strategy discussions. So, persist! Take a stand, argue a side, and take risks. Finally, draw on strategy tools, frameworks, concepts, theories, and data/quantitative analysis whenever possible. Don’t just say “I think they should do X.” Instead say things like, “well, the ABC framework, tells us that Ducati will be most successful compared to competitors if they [fill in the blank]. Looking at evidence from table X [evidence], my analysis suggests they should….”

 

  1. General Engagement in Class (30% of class participation score): In addition to cold call performances, I also track and assess your contributions to class discussions (with the help of our TAs and other students in the class). Quality and quantity of participation in class discussions forms the basis for this grade, and you will receive participation points at midterm and at the end of the class depending on where you fall in the student participation distribution. You can assess your grade by considering your performance relative to the average student in class, and also by reaching out to the TAs for feedback. In some cases, I also penalize people who are contributing a large quantity of low value-add comments. A high-quality comment…

…reflects an accurate and thoughtful understanding of the topic and the supporting data;

…doesn’t just repeat or rephrase what others already have said;

…provides new insight or draws on relevant personal/professional experiences;

…moves the discussion in productive directions, generating follow-on discussion;

…contributes to an environment of open dialogue and informed debate;

…is guided by and reflects the principles of critical thinking; and

…is provided from a posture of Christlike charity.  

 

I want to support you in participating in class, and more importantly in fostering this important skill! If you are worried about your class contributions, please come visit me early in the semester so we can find ways to help you succeed.

 

Finally, if you are using technology in a distracting way during class, you will lose points for your (lack of) general engagement in that class session. The TA will help me track these situations. Believe it or not, I notice these things as I’m leading a case discussion, and it can be highly distracting to me and others when you are working on catching up on Slack messages with a friend during class or working on finding your next Mutual date!

 

Midterm Exam (25% of final grade): There will be one midterm exam about two-thirds of the way through the semester. This exam will consist of multiple choice, free response, and case-based questions. I will provide more details about the exam format and content approximately one month before the exam. The midterm exam is an individual exam. It will be closed book and closed note and closed to all outside help (including any form of AI). It will be timed, and I will provide reading materials in advance to give you time to read through and perform any analyses you may want in advance. This exam is typically administered via Canvas with Proctorio as a virtual proctor.

 

Team Strategy Project (30% of final grade): You will work with your team during the semester to complete a strategy project for a company of your choosing. I have criteria posted on Canvas that will help you in choosing a company and in completing the project. We will also discuss best practices in class. This will account for a significant portion of your grade (30%) and will be graded by me with input from your peers and from the class TAs. This final project, in conjunction with the exam, will function as the capstone of the course. For this project your team will apply your strategy training to a real company in real time. This is your opportunity to draw on strategy tools, frameworks, concepts, and theories to make a difference. Part of being a BYU MBA is serving and giving back, and this is an opportunity to serve and provide value to a company. I have found that often small and medium size companies benefit the most from these projects, and that they typically have only limited access to MBA-level education or outside consultants. You will be graded as a group. However, to prevent free riding, your participation will also be assessed confidentially and individually by your other group members. A low participation assessment by group members can significantly lower your final individual score for the project, AND for the class. The final deliverable is a class presentation and a slide deck (that typically includes presentation slides as well as an appendix with deeper analyses). Mid-way through the semester your team will present the first part of your analysis (leading up to the crux) to the class and receive feedback, which will then aid you as you work towards finalizing the crux and developing recommendations. Please see the assignment details on Canvas for more information on this project.

It is critical to maintain a professional environment that is conducive to class participation and discussions. I allow laptops, tablets, and cell phones to be used during class. Academic Honesty

The first injunction of the Honor Code is the call to "be honest." Students come to the university not only to improve their minds, gain knowledge, and develop skills that will assist them in their life's work, but also to build character. "President David O. McKay taught that character is the highest aim of education" (The Aims of a BYU Education, p.6). It is the purpose of the BYU Academic Honesty Policy to assist in fulfilling that aim. As your instructor I actively seek to identify and penalize cheating, including plagiarism and the use of AI on deliverables, except those that note that AI use is acceptable. For a definition of plagiarism see https://marriottschool.byu.edu/policies/classroom/. For the purposes of this class, submitting answers using or derived from ChatGPT or your other favorite generative LLM constitutes plagiarism, as this is not your work. Please do not short-circuit the MBA transformation and learning process by taking short-cuts. After you have completed your assignment (i.e., a case), and then gone through the class experience relating to it (i.e., a case discussion), I then encourage you to use AI to improve your learning retroactively. For the final project AI use is fully permitted, although you should clearly note throughout how you used AI and what pieces were derived using AI.
