Business strategy is fundamentally about helping organizations achieve superior performance relative to competitors. At its core, it is about devising and implementing strategies to create and capture value, pinpointing and addressing critical obstacles, and identifying and evaluating potential future opportunities. Strategy requires taking the perspective of a top manager to understand how profitable business strategies can be designed, implemented, and maintained. This course consequently necessitates a holistic approach that puts many other functional pieces of business into a larger context. Thus, while we will not cover marketing, accounting, operations management, and so forth directly in this course, we will draw on theories and tools in these areas to deal with bigger picture issues faced by organizations.

Pedagogically we will proceed down two distinct paths. First, we will focus on developing strategic thinking skills, including skills in problem formulation, critical thinking, and strategic decision-making. In most managerial environments managers encounter ill-structured, complex, difficult to solve problems. An over- (or under-) abundance of information and limited time make addressing these problems challenging. To generate “good” strategy, managers must be able to diagnose—to develop a problem formulation that “defines or explains the nature of the challenge… that simplifies the often-overwhelming complexity of reality in identifying certain aspects of the situation as critical.”[1] They then must think in a structured and careful way to craft a solution to this problem. The cases, final project, and class learning approach have been designed to provide opportunities to develop and refine these strategic thinking skills.

The second path develops competence in understanding and applying strategy concepts, frameworks, theories, and tools used to formulate and evaluate business strategy. Without this deep understanding you will constantly be reinventing the wheel, which will limit your effectiveness at designing, implementing, and maintaining sound business strategies. Strategic tools and frameworks have been developed to assist in strategic decision making and to help managers identify and organize essential information that can be used to evaluate potential actions or decisions. Our focus here will result in you learning and memorizing the language, theories, and tools of strategy.

The melding of these two paths this semester will allow us to achieve our learning objectives. We will seek to develop the ability to ask and answer the “right” questions to formulate, implement, and rejuvenate business strategies. To paraphrase Carl von Clausewitz, a great Prussian military thinker, strategy is not a field in which rules or theories can be developed that should be dogmatically followed. Instead, “theory serves talented practitioners by expanding their powers of observation and sharpening their instinctive sense of judgement in action.”

[1] Richard P Rumelt (2011), “Good Strategy, Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters,” p 77.

 Learning Objectives

At the end of this course, you should be able to:

  • Apply Strategic Theory and Tools: Employ strategic frameworks and tools to formulate, evaluate, implement, and rejuvenate business strategies.
  • Think Like a Strategist: Utilize principles of critical and strategic thinking to identify, formulate, and solve complex strategic problems both individually and in teams.
  • Analyze External Influences: Assess how external competitive environment factors influence long-term firm performance and average industry returns.
  • Evaluate Internal Resources and Activities: Examine how firm-level resources, capabilities, and activities can combine to deliver unique value.
  • Identify and Respond to Strategic Risks and Opportunities: Recognize common technological and competitive risks and opportunities and understand how firms can respond innovatively to achieve long-term advantages.
  • Communicate Strategic Ideas: Clearly articulate strategic ideas and recommendations verbally, in writing, and in presentations. Critique others’ strategic ideas constructively and effectively.
  • Collaborate Effectively: Work effectively in a team to provide value to a company.
  • Approach Strategy with Discipleship: Understand the differences in approaching strategy and strategic leadership from a position of discipleship and faith.
  • See Growth in Your Character and Faith: Notice that we have grown together in character and testimony as followers of Jesus Christ.

 

These course learning objectives coincide with the mission of the MBA program, the Marriott School of Business, and with the overall mission and aims of Brigham Young University. Please see the syllabus for additional information.
